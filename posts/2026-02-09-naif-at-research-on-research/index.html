February 9, 2026

On 29–30 January 2026, the Center for Reproducible Science and Research Synthesis (CRS) at the University of Zurich hosted the Research on Research (RoR) Event 2026. The two-day event brought together researchers, policymakers, and practitioners from across Switzerland and beyond to examine how science operates and how it can be improved. Contributions ranged from open science and reproducibility to error detection, clinical trial methods, and science policy — with a keynote by Rachel Heyard on how research on research informs assessment reforms, and an invited talk by Jack Leahy from the UK Metascience Unit.

NAIF Track 1 contributed a poster titled On the Current and Future Uses of Quantitative Scientometric Indicators (QSI) in Swiss Higher Education Institutions, authored by Moritz Mähr (ETH Library, ETH Zurich), Martin Jaekel (ZHAW Zurich University of Applied Sciences), David Johann (ETH Library, ETH Zurich), Rüdiger Mutz (CHESS, University of Zurich), and Elena Šimukovič (ZHAW Zurich University of Applied Sciences). The poster was presented during the poster flash talks and the dedicated poster session on the first day of the event.

The poster presented results from the 6th Swiss Year of Scientometrics (SYoS) workshop, which took place on 15 September 2025 with 27 stakeholders from Swiss higher education institutions, policy bodies, and international initiatives. It also outlined a planned qualitative survey targeting Swiss HEIs and relevant stakeholders such as the Swiss National Science Foundation (SNSF) and the State Secretariat for Education, Research and Innovation (SERI). The workshop and the survey together form the empirical backbone of Track 1’s work on the responsible use of quantitative indicators for research assessment.

The poster addressed four key questions. First, how firmly are quantitative scientometric indicators embedded in Swiss higher education? The findings show significant momentum in aligning with international frameworks — 24 institutions have signed the San Francisco Declaration on Research Assessment (DORA) and 16 are signatories of the Coalition for Advancing Research Assessment (CoARA). However, stakeholders continue to struggle with translating these declarations into everyday practice. A core principle emerged: indicators must be transparent, contextual, and fair, serving to complement rather than replace expert judgment.

Second, where are quantitative indicators currently used? Drawing on a case study from ETH Zurich, the poster showed that indicators are primarily applied at the organizational level — for monitoring research profiles, analysing collaboration networks, tracking open access shares, and understanding citation patterns — rather than for evaluating individual researchers. This reflects a broader shift in the community towards using metrics for institutional learning and strategic decision-making rather than for individual assessment.

Third, how important are the tools used for quantitative indicators? The workshop participants underscored that data quality is the most critical prerequisite. Metrics are often flawed or misapplied, and meaningful analysis requires drawing on multiple sources with rigorous data cleaning. Tools are only useful when they are purpose-driven: understanding user needs must come before defining the indicators. Close cooperation between data specialists and subject experts was identified as essential.

Fourth, what does the future of responsible quantitative indicators look like? The discussions pointed towards deeper integration of data, subject, and methodology specialists; a dialogue-based approach that clarifies purpose and limitations upfront; context-first design that builds use cases before defining indicators; and a broader scope that moves beyond citations to include open research data, open science practices, and media visibility.

The RoR Event provided a fitting venue for this work. Track 1’s focus on the responsible use of quantitative indicators connects directly to broader questions about how research systems can be designed to be more robust, transparent, and fair — themes that ran through the entire event programme. The poster session offered a valuable opportunity to discuss these findings with researchers working on related challenges across different disciplines and institutions.

Based on this work, NAIF will develop practical guidelines for Swiss higher education institutions to support transparent, context-specific research assessment aligned with international best practices by the end of 2026.

The poster and further information about the project are available on the NAIF website.
